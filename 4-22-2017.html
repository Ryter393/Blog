Listen To Your Feelings, But Don't Trust Them

I consider Stoicism to be a pretty important philosophy, and the Enchiridion of Epictetus should be required reading for anyone serious about improving their worldview. However, Stoicism is often misunderstood as being about not having feelings.

Having emotions is an inherent aspect of being human, and stoics are often viewed in pop culture as being cold, emotionless, or robotic. So when I give advice inspired by Stoic philosophy like don't trust your feelings, don't be ruled by your emotions, and write an article about distancing yourself from emotional reactions, I feel people may misconstrue this advice as telling people to not feel. Which is of course ridiculous, and likely impossible for all but the sociopaths among us.

However, there is an important distinction between feeling and acting. Just because we feel something doesn’t mean we have to act upon it, or even should.

I’ve noticed that many decisions which people will later regret were at the time made under the influence of strong, but invalid, emotions.

Invalid Emotions

By invalid emotions I mean those that aren’t reasonable reactions to your circumstances.

Good rule of thumb: if you wouldn’t approve of your actions without the attached emotion, then it probably isn’t a good idea. This can be difficult, and requires being honest with yourself, though often you already know the answer (“I’m going to regret this”) and the difficulty is in admitting it to yourself.

Poor decisions are often made under the influence of invalid emotions so by considering if you would think this was a good decision even without the feeling attached to it you can avoid making poor decisions and the regret that comes with them.
